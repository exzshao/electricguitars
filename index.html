An Overview - How It Works: The Science Behind Electric Guitars

Electric guitars convert the string's vibrations into electrical signals and then use the concepts of magnetism and electromagnetic induction to amplify them as sound.

1. String Vibration: Turning Energy Into Motion

When a string is either plucked or strummed, its vibrational frequency will depend on length, tension, and thickness. Such vibration produces mechanical energy which dictates the note's pitch and tone.

Thicker strings, like the low E, vibrate more slowly and produce lower notes.

Thinner strings, like the high E, vibrate more quickly and produce higher notes.

Take the low E string, remove your finger and let the string ring on its own. It sounds flat and boomy. Now place your finger on the high E string and do the same. It resonates with a musical "ping!" - a small and sharp sound.

2. Magnetic Pickup Operation: Converting Motion Into Electricity

The pickups are situated beneath the strings and at the heart of electric guitar sound reproduction:

As a string vibrates, it disrupts the magnetic field, causing variations in the strength of the magnetic flux. This agitation causes an electrical current in the coil of the pickup-a process described by Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction.

Single-coil Pickups

Bright and clear in tone, such as the Fender Stratocaster. They tend to pick up interference, hence that very distinctive hum.

Humbucker Pickups

Include two coils wired in opposite polarity, reducing interference and producing a warmer tone.

3. Signal Transmission: Carrying the Sound to Amplifiers

Thus, the electrical signal sourced from the pickups travels through the guitar's wiring to the output jack, from where it goes through a cable to the amplifier for processing and amplification.

Tone and Volume Controls:

  • The volume knob regulates the total output of the signal.
  • The tone knob filters high frequencies through a capacitor, creating a rounder, warmer sound.

As an example, the easy rolling of the tone knob gives a softer sound, which is very typical in jazz or blues music.

4. Real-World Examples of Electric Guitar Operation

Clear Tone

In the clean setting, the character of the guitar is clear and natural, representing the string vibration.

Example: The intro to "Under the Bridge" by the Red Hot Chili Peppers demonstrates the stunning character of tones produced by single-coil pickups.

Distorted Sound

The overdriven signal on the amplifiers creates that grittiness and distorted sound that packs a punch with harmonics.

Example: The great riff in "Smoke on the Water" by Deep Purple shows the rich, distorted character of humbuckers.

Performance Dynamics

Performances are loud in nature, meaning the harder or further you strike it, coupled with the vibration of the string, must be loud and severe.

Example: Unleashing light picking sequences produces mellow tones, whereas aggressive strumming creates a well of all-powerful sounds.

Electromagnetic Induction: The Science Behind Electric Guitar Pickups

In simple words, electromagnetic induction is yet another important principle through which electric guitars convert string vibration into electrical signals.

1 Magnetic Field Generation

The pick-ups are the heart and soul of every electric guitar.

  • When the string vibrates, it momentarily generates its own little magnetic field, which interacts with the original magnetic field of the pickups.
  • For example, suppose you pluck a steel string. While it vibrates, it gyrates a bit in the magnetic field and generates an electromagnetic induction process in that sector, causing slight ripples in the field.

2 String Interaction and Magnetic Flux Disturbance

Whenever the string vibrates, the distance between it and the pickup changes. Such movement upsets the magnetic field and changes the magnetic flux-or the total magnetic field passing through the coil.

Amplitude

The harder a string is plucked, the more it vibrates, this results in a louder sound output.

Frequency

The rate at which the string vibrates (the frequency) determines the pitch of sound.

Example: If you strike the low E string, its slower vibration will disturb the magnetic field at a lower frequency and produce a lower tone.

3 Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

For any variation in magnetic flux, induction of an electrical current into the coil of the pickup occurs. Faraday's Law describes this, and put simply, a variable magnetic field in a conductor produces an electric current in a conductor nearby.

The strength of the induced current depends on one thing: how fast the string is vibrating-interpreted as frequency-and the amplitude of vibration. The resulting electrical signal is a reflection of the physical vibration of the string:

Single-Coil Pickup vs. Humbucker

Single-Coil

Gives a clean, bright signal but is easily affected by noise from electromagnetic interference.

Humbucker

Wired together in opposite polarity, cancel interference and provide a fuller, warmer sound.

Electromagnetic induction thus allows electric guitars to act as a conduit between physical vibration and electric energy, thereby one of the most versatile instruments of modern music. It is this very seamless conversion that allows the electric guitar to capture the spirit of musical expression, facilitating the exploration of unlimited exploration and tonal possibilities.

Resonance in Electric Guitars

Natural Frequency

Each string on the guitar has its own natural frequency. This is the whole reason we tune guitars! When we tune a guitar, we change the tension of its strings, and we aim to match the string's natural frequency to the correct note's frequency. It's natural frequency is also affected by the thickness and the length.

String Tension

Higher tension = Higher frequency = Higher pitch

String Thickness

Thicker string = Lower frequency = Lower pitch

Frets and String Length

To play more notes than just the open strings, we use frets - the metal bands on the neck of the guitar. Pressing a string against a fret shortens its length, since the string only vibrates effectively up until where your finger presses down on it.

The Science Behind It

This is similar to the "Standing Waves on a string" example - changing the length changes the wavelength, which changes the frequency. The closer to the guitar body you press, the shorter the string length becomes, producing a higher frequency note.

Frets divide the string into specific lengths for precise notes

Electric vs Acoustic: The Resonance Difference

Acoustic Guitars

Requires a hollow body to resonate and amplify the sound from its strings

Electric Guitars

Only cares about the actual frequency of the strings - sound is captured via "pickups" and amplification is done electronically.

String Physics and Resonance

How Strings Work

Each string on the guitar has its own natural frequency determined by three key factors:

  • String thickness affects the fundamental frequency
  • String tension (adjusted when tuning)
  • String length (changed by pressing frets)

Thicker strings, like the low E, vibrate more slowly and produce lower notes. Thinner strings, like the high E, vibrate more quickly and produce higher notes.
